Why Joint Sand Washes Out and How to Fix It

When homeowners notice empty joints, the first thought is usually that the sand was poor quality. Sometimes that is part of it, but more often the real problem is process, drainage, or maintenance. In Florida, joint sand washout is usually a symptom of water movement or cleaning habits that the surface has been fighting for a while.

Learning Center HomeBack to Problems

Heavy rain and runoff are common causes

Jacksonville storms can move a surprising amount of water across a paver field. If runoff concentrates at an edge, a low spot, or a downspout discharge, joint sand can disappear faster in those sections. Homeowners often notice the problem after a big rain, but the grading or drainage issue was already there.

Aggressive pressure washing strips out sand quickly

One of the fastest ways to lose joint sand is blasting directly into the joints during cleaning. Even if the surface looks cleaner afterward, the paver system is less stable. If that sounds familiar, read can pressure washing damage pavers? for the cleaning side of the issue.

Worn sealer can leave the joints more vulnerable

When a sealed system ages, the sand may lose some of the stability it had when the surface was first finished. That does not mean sealer alone solves everything, but it is often part of the bigger picture. If your surface also looks dull or absorbs water quickly, review the signs your pavers need resealing.

The fix depends on why the sand is leaving

Simply adding new sand without correcting the cause is usually temporary. The right repair may include cleaning, re-sanding, addressing drainage, and evaluating whether the surface is due for resealing. Homeowners get the best long-term result when the entire system is looked at together instead of as separate problems.

Why it is worth addressing early

Open joints invite more movement, more weed growth, more insect activity, and a rougher overall look. Catching sand loss early is usually a low-risk maintenance correction. Waiting too long can turn it into a bigger stabilization issue.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is some sand loss normal after heavy rain?

A small amount can happen, but repeated or widespread loss usually points to a bigger issue.

Will resealing alone fix washed-out joints?

Not by itself. Missing sand usually needs to be replaced first, and the cause should be addressed.

Can gutter or irrigation runoff cause this?

Yes. Concentrated water is one of the most common causes of joint washout.

Do weeds get worse when sand washes out?

Usually yes, because open joints create easier places for growth to take hold.
